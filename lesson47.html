Lesson 47: Case Study – Aquaculture Expansion & Funding

Aquaculture is one of Nigeria’s fastest-growing agribusiness sectors due to increasing demand for fish and shrinking supply from capture fisheries. But many fish farmers operate informally and struggle to attract funding. This case study shows how one accountant helped a small aquaculture business secure funding and scale sustainably.

Business Background

  • Business Name: BlueHarvest Farms
  • Location: Ibadan, Oyo State
  • Model: Catfish production in concrete tanks
  • Capacity: 8,000 fish per cycle
  • Owner: Mr. Adewale, a former teacher turned fish farmer
  • Goal: Expand from 8,000 to 20,000 fish and enter smoked catfish export
  • Challenge: No access to credit or investor interest due to lack of financial structure

The Situation

Despite decent sales and strong market demand, BlueHarvest Farms had:

  • No formal business registration
  • No financial records beyond rough notebook entries
  • No business plan or funding proposal
  • No clarity on current profitability or return on investment

Mr. Adewale had tried applying for several agribusiness grants and even approached angel investors - but was ignored or rejected every time.

The Consultant’s Approach

A young accounting professional, interning with an agricultural foundation, stepped in with a mission: help structure BlueHarvest’s business to secure funding and manage future growth responsibly.

Step 1: Business Structuring

  • Registered the business as a limited liability company (CAC)
  • Opened a corporate bank account to separate personal and business funds
  • Set up basic accounting templates: income statement, cash flow, and inventory ledger

Step 2: Financial Analysis

  • Reconstructed past 12 months’ income and expenses using receipts and notes
  • Determined:
    • Average cost per cycle = ₦1.9 million
    • Average profit per cycle = ₦650,000
    • ROI per cycle = 34%

This showed strong profitability - exactly what funders want to see.

Step 3: Business Plan & Expansion Model

  • Designed a three-year growth plan:
    • Scale to 20,000 fish per cycle in Year 1
    • Introduce smoked catfish processing in Year 2
    • Begin exports in Year 3
  • Created a funding proposal for ₦4.5 million to support infrastructure, feed, labor, and marketing

Step 4: Funding Strategy

  • Applied to CBN AGSMEIS loan (Agribusiness/Small and Medium Enterprise Investment Scheme)
  • Submitted proposal to a local impact investor network
  • Positioned BlueHarvest for a NIRSAL Microfinance Bank loan

Step 5: Setup of Monitoring & Reporting System

  • Implemented monthly reporting using Excel
  • Designed a grant compliance checklist and budget tracker
  • Built capacity of the owner to understand financial basics and communicate them confidently

Outcome (After 6 Months)

  • Received ₦3 million AGSMEIS loan at 5% interest per annum
  • Capacity expanded from 8,000 to 14,000 fish per cycle
  • Started small-scale smoked catfish packaging for supermarkets
  • Became eligible for export support from NEPC (Nigerian Export Promotion Council)
  • Now tracks profitability, cost per kg, feed conversion ratios, and working capital clearly

Lessons for Accounting Professionals

  • Good businesses fail to grow without structure. Accountants can bridge the gap between great products and growth capital.
  • Financial clarity = Funding eligibility. Grant officers and investors don’t care about passion - they care about data and viability.
  • Simple systems are better than no systems. Even basic monthly Excel records can transform how a business operates and scales.
