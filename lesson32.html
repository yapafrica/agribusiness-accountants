Lesson 32: Biological Assets (IAS 41)

Agribusinesses are often characterized by their reliance on biological assets - living plants and animals - for production. These assets, ranging from crops to livestock, play a vital role in the operations of agricultural businesses. IAS 41 – Agriculture provides the framework for how to account for these biological assets and how they should be valued, recognized, and disclosed.

In this lesson, we’ll explore the key provisions of IAS 41 related to biological assets, how these provisions affect agribusiness financial reporting, and the challenges that accountants may face when applying these standards.

I. What Are Biological Assets?

1.1 Definition

Under IAS 41, biological assets are defined as living plants or animals. This can include:

  • Crops (e.g., maize, tomatoes, and cocoa).
  • Livestock (e.g., cattle, poultry, sheep).
  • Forests (e.g., timber-producing trees).
  • Aquatic organisms (e.g., fish, prawns in aquaculture).

The key distinction between biological assets and other assets (such as inventory) is that biological assets are living and are actively involved in the production process.

1.2 Examples of Biological Assets in Agribusiness:

  • Crops: Maize, wheat, rice, fruits, and vegetables grown for commercial sale.
  • Livestock: Cattle, poultry, sheep, and goats raised for food production or other uses.
  • Forests: Timber trees grown for commercial use (e.g., timber and paper production).
  • Aquaculture: Fish and other aquatic life raised for food, including commercial fish farms.

II. Initial Recognition of Biological Assets

2.1 Recognition Criteria

Under IAS 41, biological assets should be recognized at initial recognition based on their fair value. The standard requires that biological assets be measured at their fair value at the time they are acquired, as well as when they are harvested. However, if the fair value cannot be reliably measured, the asset is recorded at cost. This situation may arise when biological assets do not have a direct market price or there is insufficient data to establish a fair market value.

2.2 Measurement of Biological Assets

At initial recognition, biological assets must be measured at fair value less the costs to sell (such as transportation or harvest costs). The fair value of biological assets reflects what the asset is worth in the marketplace and how much a willing buyer would pay to purchase it.

2.3 Harvesting and Subsequent Measurement

Once the biological asset is harvested (e.g., crops are picked, livestock are sold), its value should be measured at fair value as of the harvest date. After harvesting, the product becomes inventory and should be measured in accordance with IAS 2 – Inventories.

III. Fair Value Measurement of Biological Assets

3.1 Importance of Fair Value

Fair value is the most important concept in IAS 41 for measuring biological assets. Unlike other accounting standards where assets are usually recorded at cost, biological assets are typically measured at their fair value, as this better reflects their actual value in use.

3.2 Challenges in Fair Value Measurement

Determining fair value for biological assets can be complex due to several factors, including:

  • Market volatility: The value of biological assets like crops and livestock is highly susceptible to market fluctuations. For example, crop prices can vary significantly due to changes in weather, supply, and demand.
  • No active market: Some biological assets, especially those that are unique or uncommon, may not have a readily available market price.
  • Estimations: In cases where market prices are unavailable, fair value is often determined using models that rely on assumptions, such as growth rates, yields, and future price forecasts.

3.3 Methods for Determining Fair Value

IAS 41 allows the use of the market approach, income approach, or cost approach to determine fair value.

  • Market approach: Uses prices and other relevant information generated by market transactions.
  • Income approach: Estimates the present value of future cash flows associated with the asset.
  • Cost approach: Uses the cost of producing or replacing the asset.

IV. Gains and Losses on Biological Assets

4.1 Biological Assets Revaluation

At the end of each reporting period, biological assets should be revalued at fair value. Any changes in the fair value of the biological asset, either increases or decreases, should be recognized in profit or loss.

4.2 Accounting for Gains and Losses

The changes in fair value result in gains or losses that are recognized directly in the income statement. For example:

  • If the fair value of livestock increases due to better market conditions, the business recognizes a gain.
  • If crop prices fall due to poor weather, the business records a loss.

4.3 Impact on Profitability

This method of recognizing changes in fair value ensures that the financial statements reflect the most up-to-date market conditions. However, it can also lead to volatility in reported profits, especially for agribusinesses with significant biological assets subject to market fluctuations.

V. Depreciation and Amortization of Biological Assets

5.1 No Depreciation for Biological Assets

Since biological assets are measured at fair value and revalued each period, they do not undergo the same depreciation process as other assets. Depreciation applies only to non-biological assets such as buildings or machinery.

For example, the cost of farm equipment (a non-biological asset) will be depreciated over time, but livestock or crops will be revalued based on fair value rather than depreciated.

VI. Biological Assets and Agricultural Produce

6.1 From Biological Asset to Agricultural Produce

The key distinction in IAS 41 is between biological assets and agricultural produce:

  • Biological assets are the living plants and animals that are actively producing or growing.
  • Agricultural produce refers to the harvested product of these assets (e.g., crops that have been harvested or livestock that have been slaughtered).

When biological assets are harvested, their status changes from being a biological asset to agricultural produce. At this point, they are subject to IAS 2 – Inventories and should be valued at cost or net realizable value, whichever is lower.

VII. Disclosure Requirements

Under IAS 41, agribusinesses must disclose specific information related to biological assets in their financial statements. These disclosures include:

  • The carrying amount of biological assets, separated by type (e.g., crops, livestock, etc.).
  • The methods and significant assumptions used to measure fair value.
  • Any changes in the fair value during the period.
