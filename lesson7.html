Lesson 7: Costing and Budgeting for Agribusiness

Costing and budgeting are the backbone of any sustainable agribusiness. Without knowing what it costs to produce a product – or what it will take to run a farming season – agribusiness owners are essentially operating blind. As an accountant, your job is to help bring financial clarity, structure, and foresight to their operations.

Understanding Costing in Agribusiness

Costing refers to calculating all expenses incurred in producing a good or service. In agriculture, this could be:

  • The cost to raise 1,000 broiler chickens
  • The cost to plant and harvest 2 hectares of maize
  • The cost to process 10 tons of cassava into garri

Accurate costing helps business owners:

  • Set profitable prices
  • Avoid wasteful spending
  • Make informed decisions about scaling

Types of Costs to Know

  1. Direct Costs: These can be directly traced to the production process.
    • Seeds, feed, fertilizer, water, chemicals, packaging, etc.
    • Labour used for planting, harvesting, feeding animals
  2. Indirect Costs (Overheads): General costs not tied to one specific activity.
    • Farm manager salaries, fuel for generators, depreciation of tools
  3. Fixed Costs: Costs that stay the same regardless of output level.
    • Land lease, equipment depreciation, salaries of permanent staff
  4. Variable Costs: Costs that change with the level of production.
    • Quantity of feed, number of hired hands, packaging materials

Common Mistakes Agribusiness Owners Make

  • Underestimating hidden costs like pest control or fuel
  • Not separating business costs from personal expenses
  • Lumping all expenses together without tracking which operation they relate to
  • Pricing based on guesswork or competitor pricing, not cost structure

Your role: Break down costs clearly and help owners identify where they’re losing money or under-pricing their products.

Budgeting for Agribusiness

A budget is a financial plan for a season, project, or year. It estimates expected income and planned expenses.

Why It Matters:

  • Helps plan funding needs (loans, grants, savings)
  • Keeps spending under control
  • Allows for realistic profit targets
  • Helps prepare for emergencies or lean periods

Key Elements in a Farm Budget:

  1. Projected Revenue:
    • Quantity x Price (e.g., 500 crates of eggs x ₦2,000)
  2. Estimated Costs:
    • All direct and indirect costs
  3. Contingency:
    • A small buffer (usually 5–10%) for unexpected costs like inflation or crop failure
  4. Expected Profit:
    • Revenue minus Total Costs

Practical Example: Poultry Broiler Budget (1000 Birds)

Item Estimated Cost (₦)
Day-old chicks (1000) 350,000
Feed (starter, grower, finisher) 600,000
Medications/Vaccines 50,000
Labour 80,000
Electricity/Water 40,000
Miscellaneous 30,000
Total Costs ₦1,150,000

Estimated Sales:
1000 birds x ₦1,800 = ₦1,800,000

Expected Profit: ₦650,000

From this, the accountant can help the farmer calculate:

  • Break-even point
  • Return on investment (ROI)
  • Profit margin per bird

Helping Clients Build Cost Discipline

Teach agribusiness owners to:

  • Track every naira spent (using logs, Excel, or mobile tools)
  • Compare budgets with actuals regularly
  • Adjust prices based on real cost data
  • Include cost considerations in every decision – new land, bigger harvest, new equipment
