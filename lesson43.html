Lesson 43: Building Trust with Agribusiness Owners

In agribusiness consulting, your technical expertise is only half the job. The other half - and often the most important - is trust.

Many agribusiness owners operate in tight-knit, informal systems. They value relationships, word-of-mouth, and consistency over credentials alone. If they don’t trust you, they won’t fully open up their books, their problems, or their ambitions.

So how do you, as a young accountant or aspiring consultant, build and maintain that trust?

I. Understand Their World Before Offering Solutions

Trust begins with understanding.

  • Visit their farms or facilities. See how they work, where they struggle, what keeps them up at night.
  • Speak their language. Don’t overwhelm with jargon. Explain “cost-benefit analysis” as “how to know if this investment is worth it.”
  • Respect their realities. You may be the expert in finance, but they are the expert in farming. Treat them as equals.

When agribusiness owners feel heard and respected, they’re more likely to listen and cooperate.

II. Keep It Confidential and Professional

Most agri-entrepreneurs are not used to sharing their financial details - some feel exposed or even embarrassed.

You earn trust by:

  • Keeping their data confidential: No matter how small the business is, never share details without permission.
  • Avoiding judgment: If their records are poor or their debts are high, your job is to help - not criticize.
  • Being consistent: Don’t promise to deliver and disappear. Show up, follow up, and be reliable.

Trust grows over time. Every missed deadline or careless word can damage that growth.

III. Start Small, Deliver Value Fast

When starting out:

  • Offer to help with something simple - maybe just setting up a daily expense tracker or computing farm input costs.
  • Give quick wins: If they see savings, clarity, or better pricing after your advice, they’ll keep you around.
  • Don’t try to change everything at once. Focus on one problem, one solution at a time.

Small, repeated value builds credibility better than any one-time “big solution.”

IV. Be Present, Even After the Work Is Done

If you only show up during reports or audits, they’ll treat you like any other “outsider.”

Instead:

  • Check in regularly (a phone call, visit, or WhatsApp message goes a long way)
  • Offer proactive insights (e.g., “There’s a new tax update you should know about…”)
  • Celebrate their wins - whether they secured a grant or hit a harvest target.

When you become part of their success story, you’re no longer just a consultant. You’re a trusted partner.

V. Be Honest About What You Know and Don’t Know

Nothing damages trust faster than pretending.

If you don’t understand a specific crop system or a funding regulation, say so - and then go learn. Agribusiness owners respect honesty far more than fake confidence.

Your humility shows professionalism. Your willingness to learn shows commitment.

VI. Case Example

Rita, a young accounting graduate, offered free weekly bookkeeping support to a poultry farmer. She didn’t just deliver spreadsheets - she explained the numbers in simple terms, helped the farmer price more accurately, and introduced her to a grant opportunity. Within months, the farmer referred three more clients to Rita - because she trusted her.

That’s how trust grows. Not through marketing. Through value, consistency, and care.
