Lesson 39: Due Diligence and Accountability for Grant Projects

Grants are not free money - they’re investments. And every serious investor conducts due diligence before releasing funds and expects accountability during and after project execution.

As an accountant or consultant in agribusiness, your role goes beyond spreadsheets. You must ensure that all due diligence expectations are met and that accountability is baked into every stage of the grant lifecycle.

I. What is Due Diligence in Grant Projects?

Due diligence is a pre-funding assessment process where the donor, investor, or grantor examines whether your organization or agribusiness is eligible, credible, and capable of managing the proposed funds responsibly.

It covers:

  • Financial capacity
  • Governance and leadership
  • Track record
  • Internal controls
  • Legal and regulatory compliance
  • Project feasibility

If you can’t pass due diligence, the funding stops before it starts.

II. Key Documents Required for Grant Due Diligence

As an accountant, you help prepare or vet these documents:

  1. Audited Financial Statements (Last 2–3 years)
  2. Organizational Profile/Registration Certificates (CAC, SCUML, etc.)
  3. Tax Clearance Certificates
  4. Board Resolutions and Governance Structures
  5. Bank Statements
  6. Budget and Work Plan for Proposed Project
  7. Organogram and Key Staff CVs
  8. Internal Control Policy Manuals
  9. Evidence of Past Project Implementation (Reports, Photos, Testimonials)

III. How to Strengthen a Business for Due Diligence

If you’re consulting or supporting a client:

  • Ensure books of account are up to date.
  • Register the business properly with relevant authorities.
  • Set up a separate bank account for grant funds.
  • Prepare clear documentation of income and expenditure.
  • Have basic internal control procedures - approval workflows, expense limits, segregation of duties.

You don’t need to be a big organization - just a transparent and structured one.

IV. Accountability in Grant Projects

Once the grant is received, accountability becomes key. It involves:

  1. Transparency in Fund Use
    • Spend on approved activities only
    • Avoid personal or unrelated expenses
  2. Regular Financial Reporting
    • Monthly/quarterly updates with supporting documents
  3. Project Milestone Tracking
    • Matching activities and outcomes with budget spend
  4. Stakeholder Communication
    • Sharing reports and outcomes with community, funders, and partners
  5. Audits and Spot Checks
    • Be audit-ready at any time

V. Tools for Ensuring Accountability

  • Chart of Accounts Specific to the Grant
  • Transaction Logs and Vouchers
  • Expenditure Tracking Sheets
  • Bank Reconciliation Statements
  • Grant Compliance Checklists
  • Audit Trails (Digital or Paper-Based)

Use simple Excel templates or accounting software (like QuickBooks or Wave) to structure things, even if the business is small.

VI. Common Red Flags for Donors

Funders will look for these warning signs during due diligence or project monitoring:

  • Missing or inconsistent financial records
  • No board or governance structure
  • Vague budgets with no justification
  • Unexplained variances in financial reports
  • Commingling grant and personal funds
  • Late or no reporting
  • Lack of documentation (receipts, contracts, timesheets)

Your job is to make sure these red flags never show up.

VII. Your Role as an Agribusiness Consultant

  • Conduct internal due diligence checks for clients before funding is sought
  • Help prepare and review documents needed for donor assessments
  • Build basic financial systems and documentation processes
  • Offer grant compliance training to client teams
  • Develop checklists and reporting templates
  • Serve as an accountability partner throughout the project
