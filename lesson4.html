Lesson 4: Types of Agribusiness Models (Farming, Processing, Distribution)

Agribusiness is not a one-size-fits-all industry. Different businesses operate at different points of the value chain and each model has unique risks, revenue patterns, cost structures, and accounting needs.

Understanding these models helps you tailor your approach when working with agribusiness clients.

1. Farming Enterprises

What they do:

These are the producers: crop farmers, poultry farms, fish farms, piggery operations, and livestock rearers.

Revenue model:

Income is usually seasonal, depending on harvests or production cycles. For example, a maize farmer might earn bulk revenue once or twice a year.

Typical costs include:

  • Seeds or fingerlings
  • Fertilizers, feed, medication
  • Labour (often informal)
  • Water, land leasing, basic tools

Accounting focus:

  • Cash flow planning (especially to survive off-season)
  • Cost of production per hectare, bird, fish pond, etc.
  • Yield measurement and variance analysis
  • Asset management for tools, tractors, and farm infrastructure

Key risk: Market prices fluctuate, and losses from pests, weather, or poor planning can wipe out an entire seasonâ€™s income.

2. Processing Businesses

What they do:

These businesses add value by transforming raw materials into finished or semi-finished goods. Examples include:

  • Turning cassava into garri or starch
  • Making palm oil from palm fruit
  • Processing soybeans into livestock feed

Revenue model:

Can be more stable with higher margins. Products are often sold to wholesalers, retailers, or directly to consumers.

Typical costs include:

  • Raw material procurement
  • Labour (skilled/unskilled)
  • Energy and fuel
  • Packaging and labeling
  • Equipment maintenance

Accounting focus:

  • Cost of production per unit
  • Fixed vs variable costs
  • Waste reduction and efficiency tracking
  • Break-even analysis

Key risk: High initial investment in equipment. Poor inventory or quality control can quickly erode profit margins.

3. Distribution and Logistics

What they do:

These players move goods from farms or processors to end-users: markets, supermarkets, exporters, restaurants, etc. They may own trucks, warehouses, or manage logistics for third parties.

Revenue model:

Revenue comes from delivery contracts, commissions, or mark-ups on bulk purchases.

Typical costs include:

  • Transportation (fuel, driver wages, maintenance)
  • Warehousing
  • Handling and packaging
  • Delays, spoilage, or damages in transit

Accounting focus:

  • Fleet and route cost tracking
  • Profit margin per delivery or product
  • Inventory turnover and loss tracking
  • Payment terms and receivables

Key risk: Delays, fuel price hikes, and perishables spoilage can eat into profits.

Combining Models: Integrated Agribusinesses

Some businesses run across multiple stages. For example, a poultry farm might hatch chicks, raise them, slaughter, package, and deliver to restaurants. These integrated models offer better profit control but also require more capital, structure, and expert financial management.

As an accountant, your challenge is to understand each layer and set up systems that capture the right information at each point.
