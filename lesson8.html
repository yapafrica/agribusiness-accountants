Lesson 8: Inventory Management in Agricultural Enterprises

Inventory is everything in agribusiness. Whether it’s seeds waiting to be planted, feeds for livestock, or bags of harvested produce ready for sale — poor inventory management can mean losses, waste, or missed opportunities.

As an accountant in the agribusiness space, understanding how to track and control inventory is key to helping your clients run a profitable and efficient operation.

What is Inventory in Agribusiness?

Inventory refers to all the physical goods a business owns that are involved in the production and sale of its output. In agriculture, this could include:

  • Raw materials: seeds, fertilizers, pesticides, livestock feed, water
  • Work-in-progress: growing crops or animals not yet ready for sale
  • Finished goods: harvested crops, processed products, livestock ready for market
  • Consumables: packaging materials, fuel, veterinary supplies
  • Tools and inputs: hoes, sprayers, feeding trays (often tracked separately as assets)

Why Inventory Management is Critical

Poor inventory control can lead to:

  • Wastage due to expiry, spoilage, or theft
  • Overstocking, which ties up cash flow
  • Understocking, which leads to production delays or lost sales
  • Inaccurate financial reporting

Good inventory management ensures:

  • Continuous production without interruptions
  • Better decision-making on restocking or sales
  • Improved cash flow and cost control
  • Accurate valuation for financial statements and grant proposals

Inventory Challenges in Agribusiness

  1. Perishability: Fresh produce and animal products can expire quickly if not properly stored or sold on time.
  2. Lack of technology: Many small agribusinesses still use manual tracking or no system at all.
  3. Seasonality: Some items are stocked once a year (e.g., fertilizers), while others are sold all year round.
  4. Pilferage: Poor oversight often leads to staff theft or product misuse.

Key Inventory Records Accountants Should Recommend

Encourage clients to keep these basic records:

  • Stock Ledger: Item, quantity in/out, date, balance
  • Bin Card: Quantity received, issued, balance (placed at storage location)
  • Stock Requisition Form: Staff requests for inventory use
  • Stock Valuation Record: Value of inventory on hand at any time

Inventory Valuation Methods

For financial reporting and pricing, inventory must be valued correctly. The main methods include:

  1. FIFO (First-In, First-Out): Oldest inventory is sold first. Common for perishable goods.
  2. LIFO (Last-In, First-Out): Most recent inventory is sold first. Less common in agriculture, but sometimes used where prices are rising.
  3. Weighted Average: Average cost of all inventory is used. Useful when items are mixed or hard to separate.

Example:

  • Bought 50 bags of fertilizer at ₦10,000
  • Later bought 50 bags at ₦12,000

Weighted Average Cost = (₦10,000×50 + ₦12,000×50) / 100 = ₦11,000 per bag

Role of Accountants in Inventory Management

  • Design simple inventory tracking templates (paper or Excel)
  • Help clients do periodic stock counts and reconcile with records
  • Analyze inventory turnover (how fast stock is sold/used)
  • Identify dead stock and suggest timely liquidation
  • Ensure correct inventory valuation on financial statements

Tools for Inventory Tracking

Start simple:

  • Inventory notebooks for small farmers
  • Excel templates for growing agribusinesses
  • Inventory management apps (like FarmERP, FarmDrive, Agrivi) for larger or digital-ready enterprises

Encourage regular checks: weekly or monthly physical counts to avoid surprises at year-end.
