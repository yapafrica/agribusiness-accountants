Lesson 6: Farm Operations and Record-Keeping

Understanding farm operations is critical for any accountant looking to support agribusinesses. It’s not just about crops or animals - it’s about managing inputs, labour, assets, and risk in a way that generates profit. And without proper record-keeping, it’s all just guesswork.

Let’s break it down.

What Are Farm Operations?

Farm operations are the daily activities that keep an agricultural enterprise running. These vary based on the type of farm - crop farming, poultry, livestock, aquaculture, etc. - but the core principles are similar:

Common Operations Include:

  • Land preparation and planting
  • Feeding and care of animals
  • Irrigation and fertilization
  • Pest and disease control
  • Harvesting or sales of livestock
  • Maintenance of tools and equipment
  • Labour scheduling and wages
  • Inventory management (feeds, seeds, chemicals)

Each activity involves time, resources, and money - this is where you, the accountant, come in.

Why Record-Keeping Matters

Many farmers operate informally, without structured records. They may know they “made some profit,” but they can't tell:

  • Where the money went
  • Which crop or animal brought in more returns
  • How much it costs to produce per unit
  • Whether they can afford to scale

Without data, they can’t attract funding, track growth, or make sound decisions.

Key Records Every Farm Should Keep

Here are the basic records every farm needs, and what they’re used for:

1. Input Records

Track: seeds, fertilizers, chemicals, water, feed, etc.
Why? Helps determine input cost per season and avoid waste or stockouts.

2. Labour Records

Track: workers, tasks, wages, and working hours.
Why? Labour is one of the biggest farm costs, especially during planting and harvesting.

3. Production/Yield Records

Track: crop harvests or animal growth (e.g., kg of maize per hectare, number of eggs per bird).
Why? Links effort to output, helps in yield forecasting and performance review.

4. Sales Records

Track: all sales transactions - what was sold, to whom, when, and for how much.
Why? Essential for calculating revenue and profit margins.

5. Expense Records

Track: transport, repairs, marketing, and daily operational costs.
Why? Allows the farmer to know total expenses and calculate net profit accurately.

6. Asset Records

Track: land, machinery, tools, livestock, buildings, etc.
Why? Useful for valuation, maintenance scheduling, and capital planning.

How Accountants Can Help

As a professional, you should:

  • Design simple record templates (not overly technical)
  • Train farm owners or staff on how to fill them out
  • Collect and analyze the data regularly
  • Use the data to prepare reports, forecast cash flows, or assess financing needs

Even a small farm can benefit from structured recordkeeping - and your guidance can be the difference between barely surviving and running a scalable enterprise.

Tools You Can Introduce

Start with basic tools, especially for farmers with low digital literacy:

  • Paper logbooks or notebooks with labeled sections
  • Excel spreadsheets for small farms with access to a laptop
  • Simple mobile apps (like Farm Logs, Agrikore, or Hello Tractor) for tech-savvy farmers
