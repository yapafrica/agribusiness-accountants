Fish is a major source of protein in Nigeria – and demand far exceeds supply. Nigeria consumes over 3.6 million metric tonnes of fish annually, but local production only accounts for around 1.2 million metric tonnes, creating a wide supply gap that imports try to fill. That gap represents a massive opportunity for local fisheries and aquaculture.

As an accountant, understanding how fish farming works, what it costs, and how it generates profit is critical for supporting clients or building your own agribusiness advisory niche.

What is Fisheries and Aquaculture?

  • Fisheries refer to the capture of fish from natural water bodies (rivers, lakes, oceans).
  • Aquaculture is the controlled farming of aquatic organisms – especially fish like catfish and tilapia – in tanks, ponds, or cages.

Aquaculture is more scalable, sustainable, and profitable when done right. It also gives more control over water quality, feeding, and harvesting schedules – making it appealing for investors and entrepreneurs.

Types of Aquaculture in Nigeria

  1. Pond Culture – The most common; fish are raised in dug-out ponds
  2. Tank Systems – Uses plastic or concrete tanks, ideal for urban areas
  3. Cage Culture – Fish are farmed in cages placed in rivers or lakes
  4. Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) – Advanced, indoor tech for water recycling and temperature control

Most Farmed Fish Species

  • Catfish – Dominates the local market due to high demand and fast growth
  • Tilapia – Popular in coastal and export markets
  • Clarias hybrids, Heterobranchus, and Hetero-Clarias species – Engineered for better growth and resistance

Business and Financial Dynamics

Fish farming involves high upfront investment but relatively short production cycles – typically 4–6 months for catfish. Key cost areas include:

  • Fingerlings or juveniles
  • Feed (up to 65% of total cost)
  • Water management
  • Medication and disease control
  • Labor and security
  • Processing and packaging (optional)

As an accountant, your role includes:

  • Preparing cost-benefit analysis
  • Managing cash flow (feed costs are ongoing, income comes later)
  • Tracking survival and growth rates
  • Calculating cost per kg of fish produced
  • Supporting pricing decisions and break-even analysis

Revenue Streams in Aquaculture

  1. Fresh Fish Sales – Direct to consumers, hotels, restaurants
  2. Processed Fish – Smoked or dried fish for longer shelf life and better margins
  3. Fingerling Production – Selling young fish to other farmers
  4. Feed Production and Distribution – High-profit margin segment
  5. Consulting or Training – For experienced players

Each of these revenue streams has its own costing, risks, and record-keeping needs.

Risks and Challenges

  • Water quality management – Poor water = sick or dead fish
  • Disease outbreaks
  • Feed price volatility
  • Market glut during peak seasons
  • Insecurity and theft – Especially in rural farms

Accountants help mitigate these risks by maintaining solid records, budgeting accurately, and advising on insurance or diversification strategies.

Compliance and Standards

For fish farmers targeting formal markets or exports, proper documentation and certifications are key:

  • Nigerian Institute for Oceanography and Marine Research (NIOMR)
  • Federal Department of Fisheries (FDF)
  • NAFDAC registration for packaged fish products
  • Good Aquaculture Practices (GAP) compliance

Well-documented records help meet traceability and food safety requirements.
