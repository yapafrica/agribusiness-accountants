Lesson 18: Government and Private Sector Funding Initiatives

In a country like Nigeria, where agriculture contributes over 25% to GDP and employs more than one-third of the population, both the government and private sector recognize the critical role of agribusiness in driving food security, jobs, and export growth. As a result, several targeted initiatives exist to finance and support this sector. As an aspiring agribusiness accountant or consultant, understanding these initiatives is crucial. You'll often be the bridge between your client and these funding sources.

I. Government-Led Funding Initiatives

Government programs tend to focus on job creation, food security, and financial inclusion - especially for women, youth, and rural farmers.

1. Anchor Borrowers’ Programme (ABP) – CBN

  • Target: Smallholder farmers producing strategic crops (rice, maize, wheat, cotton, etc.)
  • How It Works: Farmers are linked with off-takers who guarantee purchase. CBN funds are disbursed through Deposit Money Banks and Microfinance Banks.
  • Benefits: Inputs (seeds, fertilizer, chemicals) and cash support.
  • Role of Accountants: Help in farmer group registration, record-keeping, and loan documentation.

2. Agribusiness/Small and Medium Enterprise Investment Scheme (AGSMEIS) – CBN/NIRSAL

  • Loan Size: Up to ₦10 million
  • Interest: 9% per annum
  • Tenure: Up to 7 years, with moratorium
  • Eligible Ventures: Crop production, livestock, aquaculture, agro-processing, input supply.
  • Requirement: Training from a CBN-accredited Enterprise Development Institute.
  • Your Support: Preparing financial projections, business plans, and repayment plans.

3. NIRSAL Microfinance Bank Initiatives

  • Product Lines: Agric loans, working capital, salary loans
  • Special Focus: Credit guarantees for farmers to reduce lender risk
  • Programmes:
    • NIRSAL Agro Geo-Cooperative Formation
    • NIRSAL Climate-Smart Agriculture Financing
  • Your Role: Guide farmers and cooperatives in forming credible geo-clusters and maintaining audit-ready records.

4. National Youth Investment Fund (NYIF)

  • Target: Youth (18–35 years) in agriculture and related value chains
  • Loan: Up to ₦3 million at 5% interest
  • Components: Training, funding, mentorship
  • Advantage: Open to startups and micro-enterprises

5. Bank of Agriculture (BOA)

  • Offers direct loans to:
    • Farmers
    • Agro-processors
    • Agri-exporters
  • Usually backed by: Land, machinery, or group guarantees
  • Known for: Low-interest rates and flexible repayment structures
  • Accountant’s Advantage: Clean records = smoother application process

6. State-Level Funding Programs

  • Examples:
    • Lagos Agripreneur Programme
    • Kano Commercial Agriculture Development Project
    • Oyo Youth in Agribusiness Initiative
  • Keep an eye on state government portals or Ministry of Agriculture pages.

II. Private Sector and Donor-Led Initiatives

These are often grant-based or investment-linked, targeting innovation, youth, women, climate-smart agriculture, or export-driven businesses.

1. Tony Elumelu Foundation (TEF)

  • Support: $5,000 grant + training and mentoring
  • Target: African entrepreneurs in various sectors, including agriculture
  • Annual: Opens once a year
  • Role of Accountants: Assist with compelling business and financial proposals

2. Mastercard Foundation – Young Africa Works

  • Partners: NIRSAL, EFInA, Agric-focused NGOs
  • Target: Youth-led agribusinesses, particularly in rural areas
  • Form: Grants, technical support, business training

3. African Development Bank (AfDB) – ENABLE Youth Programme

  • Focused on youth agribusiness incubation
  • Offers training, access to finance, and mentorship
  • Works through national agricultural institutions

4. AGRA (Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa)

  • Grants to support women-led and climate-smart agribusinesses
  • Programs: WAYA (Women Agripreneurs of the Year Awards), PASS (Partnership for Seed System Development)

5. GIZ (German Development Agency)

  • Supports smallholder farmers through:
    • Technical support
    • Access to finance
    • Market linkage platforms
  • You as an Accountant: Help ensure transparency and traceability in financial records

6. NEPC (Nigerian Export Promotion Council)

  • Programs: Youth in Agrifood Export Development
  • Support: Training, access to international buyers, and grants
  • Targeted at: Export-oriented agri-businesses (e.g., cocoa, shea, hibiscus, ginger)

7. Impact Investors and Venture Funds

  • Interested in innovative, scalable agri-tech or agro-processing businesses
  • Look for:
    • Clean financials
    • Impact metrics (job creation, climate resilience, inclusion)
    • Scalable operations
  • Examples: Acumen, Root Capital, Sahel Capital, Heifer International

III. How to Make Agribusinesses Fund-Ready

As a consultant or accountant, help your clients with:

  • Business planning and forecasting
  • Record-keeping and financial statements
  • Understanding and applying for the right program
  • Preparing grant applications and investor decks
  • Building cooperative structures or clusters when required
