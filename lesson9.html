Lesson 9: Agricultural Supply Chain and Logistics

Agribusiness doesn’t end at the farm gate. From the moment a crop is harvested or an animal is processed, a new journey begins — moving products from farm to table, factory, or export terminal. This journey is what we call the agricultural supply chain, and logistics is the engine that keeps it moving.

For accountants, understanding this system is essential. It helps you advise clients on cost control, efficiency, pricing, and risk management at every stage of the value chain.

What is the Agricultural Supply Chain?

The agricultural supply chain refers to all the steps involved in producing, processing, distributing, and selling agricultural goods. It includes:

  1. Input Supply – Seeds, fertilizer, feed, equipment
  2. Production – Crop cultivation, livestock farming, aquaculture
  3. Post-Harvest Handling – Cleaning, grading, packaging
  4. Processing – Turning raw produce into finished or semi-finished products
  5. Storage – Temporary holding before transport or sale
  6. Distribution/Logistics – Transporting goods to markets, retailers, or processors
  7. Retail/Export – Selling to consumers or foreign markets

Each stage presents financial decisions — and opportunities for accountants to add value.

What is Logistics in Agribusiness?

Logistics refers to the planning and coordination of how goods, materials, and information move through the supply chain. In agriculture, it includes:

  • Transport of produce, livestock, or inputs
  • Storage facilities (warehouses, silos, cold rooms)
  • Inventory coordination
  • Delivery scheduling and cost tracking

Poor logistics can destroy value. For example, late delivery of vegetables could lead to spoilage and lost sales. Delays in feed supply could affect livestock growth and market timing.

Key Challenges in Agricultural Logistics in Nigeria

  1. Poor Rural Infrastructure: Many farms lack access to good roads. According to the Nigerian Institute of Transport Technology, over 60% of rural roads are in poor condition, increasing cost and delay.
  2. Inadequate Storage Facilities: The Nigerian Stored Products Research Institute estimates that up to 40% of food is lost post-harvest due to poor storage and handling.
  3. High Transportation Costs: Due to fuel prices, vehicle maintenance, and insecurity. These costs eat into farmer profits.
  4. Fragmented Supply Chains: Many smallholders operate in isolation, making it hard to consolidate and scale distribution efficiently.
  5. Lack of Cold Chain Infrastructure: For perishable items like fish, milk, and vegetables, absence of cold storage during transit leads to high losses.

Opportunities for Accountants in the Supply Chain

As an accountant, you can help agribusinesses:

  • Track logistics costs and assess cost-effectiveness of different routes or transport modes
  • Evaluate suppliers and negotiate better procurement terms
  • Budget for warehousing and storage during harvest seasons
  • Advise on outsourcing vs. owning logistics assets
  • Monitor inventory in transit and losses from handling or delays
  • Analyze supply chain risks and recommend insurance or mitigation strategies

You can also help position businesses for grants or funding to invest in logistics solutions (e.g., refrigerated trucks, solar cold rooms).

Tech Solutions Emerging in Agri Logistics

Several startups and digital platforms are solving logistics problems in agriculture:

  • ThriveAgric – Links farmers to markets and finance
  • ColdHubs – Provides solar-powered cold storage for perishables
  • Crop2Cash – Digitizes payments and records in the value chain
  • Hello Tractor – A shared economy model for tractor usage and tracking

Understanding these tools helps you guide clients into smarter, tech-enabled decisions.
